Pre-Move Planning

A successful relocation begins 6–12 months before you fly. Here is exactly what to prepare — tailored for both EU and non-EU citizens moving to Valencia.

6–12 moLead time for non-EU citizens
3–6 moLead time for EU/EEA citizens
€1,800–2,800Est. monthly budget (family of 4)

Documents to Prepare

Many documents require official Spanish translation (traducción jurada) and an Apostille stamp — allow 4–8 weeks for both:

  • Valid passport — minimum 1 year remaining validity recommended
  • Birth certificates for all family members (apostilled)
  • Marriage certificate, if applicable (apostilled)
  • Criminal background check from country of residence (apostilled, issued within 3–6 months of application)
  • Proof of sufficient financial means — bank statements, pension letters, investment income
  • Comprehensive private health insurance — mandatory for all visa applications
  • Biometric passport-size photographs

Visa Options for Non-EU Citizens

  • Non-Lucrative Visa (NLV): Most popular for retirees and those with passive income. Financial requirement: approx. €28,800+/year per applicant (400% IPREM, 2024 — updated annually). Applied for at your Spanish Consulate before travel.
  • Digital Nomad Visa (DNV): Introduced January 2023 (Ley 28/2022). For remote workers with non-Spanish employers. Income requirement: 200% of Spain’s minimum wage (approx. €2,646/month gross, 2024).
  • Golden Visa: For real estate investment of €500,000+ unencumbered. Grants immediate full-family residency rights.
  • EU/EEA citizens: No visa required. Register in Spain once you arrive using the EU Citizen Certificate process.

Shipping & Logistics

  • Sea freight from North America: 4–6 weeks transit; book 2–3 months in advance
  • Sea freight from Northern Europe: 1–2 weeks; road freight also a common option
  • EU citizens importing personal effects: Duty-free if items are your own, over 6 months old, and you are establishing habitual residence in Spain
  • Vehicle import: EU-registered vehicles can be driven in Spain; non-EU vehicles require ITV inspection and DGT re-registration

Cost of Living Snapshot — Valencia (2024)

  • Typically 30–40% more affordable than London, Amsterdam, or Zurich
  • Monthly public transport pass (Abono): approx. €24
  • Family restaurant set lunch (menú del día) for 2: €20–35
  • Monthly groceries for a family of 4: €400–600
  • Private health insurance: €80–200/month per adult
  • Utilities (electricity, water, internet) for 3-bed apartment: €120–220/month

Christa’s tip: The most common mistake is underestimating Spanish bureaucracy timelines. Always build in an extra 4–8 weeks buffer for appointments. We begin booking citas previas the moment you engage us.

Legal & Formalities

Spain’s bureaucracy has a reputation — but it is completely navigable with the right guide. Here is what every new resident needs, and in what order to get it.

4–12 wksTypical NIE appointment wait time
€10.60NIE application fee (Tasa 790/012)
Day 1Best time to start empadronamiento

NIE, TIE & EU Certificate — What Is What?

  • NIE (Número de Identidad de Extranjero): A tax identification number assigned to all foreigners in Spain. Not a residency permit. Required for opening bank accounts, signing leases, buying property, and virtually any official transaction. Both EU and non-EU citizens need one.
  • TIE (Tarjeta de Identidad de Extranjero): The physical residency card for non-EU citizens. Issued after your visa is approved and you arrive in Spain. Initially valid 1 year; renewed for 2 years, then 5 years (long-term residency).
  • EU Citizen Certificate (Certificado de Registro de Ciudadano de la UE): EU/EEA nationals register at the Oficina de Extranjería or a national police station and receive a green A4 certificate confirming legal residency. This also contains your NIE number.

Empadronamiento — Municipal Registration

Registration at your local Ayuntamiento is the cornerstone of life in Spain. Without it you cannot access public healthcare, enrol children in local schools, or apply for most administrative services.

  • Register at your local Ayuntamiento or Junta Municipal (district office)
  • In Valencia city: book via Valencia.es (the City Council website)
  • Documents required: valid passport + original signed rental contract or property deed
  • Document received: Volante de Empadronamiento (registration certificate)
  • Usually processed same day or within 5 working days
  • Update your empadronamiento each time you move to a new address

Banking in Spain

A Spanish bank account requires your NIE. Use a fintech card (Revolut, N26, Wise) for the first weeks while your NIE is processed.

  • BBVA: Most expat-friendly; strong English-language online banking; widespread branches
  • CaixaBank: Largest branch network in Spain; good mobile app
  • Sabadell: Strong presence in the Valencian Community; known for good customer service
  • Standard fees: €0–20/month (often free with salary direct deposit or minimum balance)
  • Documents needed: passport, NIE, empadronamiento certificate, proof of income

Health Insurance

  • Public system (Sistema Nacional de Salud): Accessible once empadronado and contributing to Social Security. Card in Valencia: Tarjeta SIP.
  • EU/UK pensioners: Use an S1 form from your home country to access Spanish public healthcare directly.
  • Private insurance (required for visa applicants): Major providers — Adeslas, Sanitas, Asisa, Cigna International. Approx. €60–180/month per adult.
  • Valencia public hospitals: Hospital La Fe, Hospital Clínico Universitari, Hospital Dr. Peset

Key reality: All Spanish government appointments (cita previa) are booked online via the Sede Electrónica or Extranjería websites. Slots disappear within minutes of release. We monitor and book these the moment you engage us.

Valencia Neighbourhood Guide

  • Ruzafa / Russafa: Valencia’s trendiest neighbourhood. International restaurants, independent cafés, lively weekend life. Most popular with expats and young professionals. 1-bed: €850–1,200/month.
  • El Carmen (Ciutat Vella): The medieval old town. Narrow streets, Gothic architecture, tapas bars, galleries. Vibrant but noisy on weekends. 1-bed: €750–1,100/month.
  • L’Eixample / Gran Vía: Wide leafy boulevards, elegant architecture, quieter feel. Excellent transport links. 1-bed: €900–1,300/month.
  • Benimaclet: Bohemian university neighbourhood. Strong community spirit, local markets, international atmosphere. More affordable — 1-bed: €650–900/month.
  • El Cabanyal: Historic fishing quarter with beach access. Rapidly gentrifying, great value relative to location. 1-bed: €700–1,050/month.
  • Campanar, Patraix, Malilla: Quieter residential family areas. More space, good schools, calmer environment. 2-bed: €850–1,200/month.

What Landlords Require

  • Valid NIE number — no NIE means no rental contract can be signed
  • 3–6 months of bank statements showing sufficient income
  • Work contract, pension certificate, or proof of recurring income
  • Previous landlord references — helpful, not always mandatory
  • Security deposit: legally capped at 1 month rent (Ley 29/1994); landlords may request 1–2 additional months as a guarantee
  • For self-employed or foreign income: a personal guarantor (aval) may be requested

Spanish Rental Law — Key Facts (LAU)

Spain’s Ley de Arrendamientos Urbanos provides strong tenant protections:

  • Minimum rental period: 5 years if landlord is a private individual; 7 years if landlord is a company (RDL 7/2019)
  • Annual rent increases capped at the official CPI index during the contract term
  • Landlord cannot terminate during the minimum period without serious legal cause
  • Tenant may exit after 6 months with 30 days’ written notice (early exit penalty may apply)

Rental Price Ranges — Valencia (2024)

  • Studio apartment: €550–800/month
  • 1-bedroom apartment: €750–1,100/month
  • 2-bedroom apartment: €950–1,500/month
  • 3-bedroom apartment: €1,200–2,000/month
  • Villa / townhouse with garden (peri-urban): €1,500–3,500/month

Market reality: Popular flats in Ruzafa and El Cabanyal receive 5–15 enquiries per day. Having all documents ready to commit immediately is the difference between getting the home and losing it. We prepare your full dossier in advance so you can say yes on the spot.

School Search

Valencia has an outstanding range of schools from free public options to world-class international programmes. We find the right fit — not just the most expensive name on a list.

3 typesPublic, Concertado & International
Feb–AprEnrollment window for September
€0–18kAnnual fees per child (full range)

The Three School Systems

  • Colegios Públicos (State Schools) — Free: Funded by the Spanish government. Instruction is in Spanish and Valencian. High academic standards. Children typically achieve fluency within 6–12 months through immersion. Priority places allocated by residential proximity (zonas de escolarización).
  • Colegios Concertados (Semi-Private) — Low/No Fees: Partly state-funded. Many offer bilingual or trilingual programmes (Spanish/Valencian/English). Often Catholic in origin but academically strong and open to all. Fees typically €0–200/month.
  • Colegios Privados / Internacionales — Fee-Paying: Fully private. British (Cambridge/A-Levels), American (AP), IB, German, or French curricula. English-medium instruction. Fees: approx. €5,000–18,000/year. Ideal if your family may relocate again or children’s Spanish is limited.

International Schools in Valencia

  • The British School of Valencia: Cambridge Curriculum and A-Levels. Ages 3–18. Located in Urbanización La Cañada (Paterna). Fees from approx. €7,000–14,000/year.
  • American School of Valencia (ASV): US curriculum with Advanced Placement (AP) courses. Ages 3–18. Located in Godella. Fees from approx. €8,000–16,000/year.
  • Colegio Alemán de Valencia: German national curriculum. Ages 3–18. Recognised by the German government. For German-speaking families.
  • Colegio Internacional Menesiano: IB Primary Years + Spanish curriculum. Ages 3–18. Bilingual Spanish/English. Competitively priced.
  • Colegio Los Olmos: Well-regarded private Spanish school with strong trilingual programme (Spanish, English, French) and rich extracurricular activities.

Enrollment Timeline

  • October–December: Research phase — visit schools, attend open days, make shortlists
  • February–April: Official state school enrollment period (Conselleria d’Educació process)
  • January–April: International school application window (many accept year-round)
  • June–July: State school placement letters issued
  • September (2nd week typically): Academic year begins
  • Mid-year entry: readily accepted at international schools; state schools require Conselleria d’Educació approval

Language & Curriculum Notes

  • Valencia is officially bilingual: Spanish (Castellano) and Valencian (Valencià) are co-official since 1982
  • Public schools teach in both; some programmes are majority-Valencian, others majority-Spanish
  • Children aged 5–12 typically achieve conversational Spanish within 6–12 months of full immersion
  • Spanish schools follow the LOMLOE national curriculum (2022 education law)
  • International schools follow their own national or IB curricula, recognised globally

Intercultural Coaching

The paperwork is one thing. Feeling genuinely at home is another. Understanding Valencia’s rhythm, language, customs, and community is what makes you thrive — not just arrive.

2Co-official languages (Spanish + Valencian)
9–11pmWhen Valencianos typically eat dinner
UNESCOLas Fallas heritage status since 2016

The Language Situation

  • Spanish (Castellano) is understood and spoken by everyone in Valencia
  • Valencian (Valencià) is a Romance language closely related to Catalan; co-official in the Comunitat Valenciana since 1982
  • Government communications, school materials, and street signage often appear in Valencian
  • Speaking even basic Valencian phrases is warmly received and opens doors socially
  • Spanish language schools in Valencia: EOI Valencia (state-funded, very affordable), CCOO, Berlitz, private tutors
  • Most expats achieve conversational Spanish within 6–12 months with regular practice

Daily Life & Rhythms

  • Breakfast: Light — coffee and a pastry at the bar, typically 7:30–9am
  • Lunch (La Comida): The main meal, 2–4pm. Most restaurants offer a menú del día: starter + main + dessert + drink for €10–16
  • Dinner (La Cena): Typically 9–11pm. Earlier is considered unusual by local standards
  • Siesta: Smaller independent shops may close 2–5pm; supermarkets and chains generally do not
  • Sundays: Most independent shops closed; family lunch culture very strong; large supermarkets open until 3pm
  • Greetings: Two cheek kisses between women and mixed groups; handshake between men in business settings
  • Tipping: Not obligatory; rounding up or leaving €1–2 is appreciated in restaurants

Valencia’s Cultural Calendar

  • Las Fallas (1–19 March): Valencia’s defining festival. Enormous satirical sculptures displayed citywide, burned on the final night (La Cremà). Daily mascletà (daytime pyrotechnics) at 2pm from 1–19 March. UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage since 2016.
  • Semana Santa (March/April): Holy Week. Solemn and spectacular processions throughout the city.
  • La Nit de Sant Joan (23 June): Midsummer night celebrated on the beach with bonfires and fireworks.
  • La Tomatina (Last Wednesday of August, Buñol): The world-famous tomato battle — approx. 150,000 kg of tomatoes. Located 39 km from Valencia. Tickets required and sell out months ahead.
  • Fiestas de Barrio (June–September): Each neighbourhood holds its own summer festival — the single best way to integrate with local community.
  • Día de la Comunitat Valenciana (9 October): Valencian national day. Grand parade, floral offerings at the Cathedral, concerts citywide.

Building Your Community

  • Valencia has one of Spain’s largest expat communities — particularly British, Dutch, German, and French nationals
  • Key expat networks: InterNations Valencia, Valencia Expats (Facebook), numerous Meetup groups
  • Pádel clubs, cycling groups, and running clubs are among the best ways to meet both locals and internationals
  • Intercambio (language exchange) events: practise Spanish while helping others with English — widely available in cafés across the city
  • The Turia Gardens (9km linear park through the city) is Valencia’s greatest social meeting place

From experience: When I arrived in Valencia in 2004, I initially stayed close to the expat bubble — comfortable, but limiting. The families who truly thrive are those who lean into Valencian life: the late dinners, the fiestas, the neighbourhood rhythms. The first year in a new country is full of surprises — tax deadlines, licence exchanges, card renewals. We stay beside you for every milestone so nothing falls through the cracks.

What We Help With in Year One

  • TIE (residency card) renewal reminders and appointment booking
  • Empadronamiento updates when you move to a new address
  • Introduction to a trusted gestoría (Spanish accountant) for tax and administrative matters
  • Driving licence exchange at the DGT — non-EU licences must be exchanged within 6 months of establishing residency
  • Vehicle ITV (technical inspection) and DGT re-registration assistance
  • School year transitions: uniform suppliers, after-school activity registration, parent group introductions
  • Healthcare: GP registration at local Centro de Salud, specialist referrals, pharmacy guidance
  • Emergency contacts: trusted plumbers, electricians, locksmiths, emergency interpreters
  • Mail assistance: translating official letters from Hacienda, Seguridad Social, or Ayuntamiento

Tax Residency — The Essentials

You become a Spanish tax resident if you spend more than 183 days per year in Spain — a fact many new arrivals overlook:

  • As a tax resident you must file a Spanish Declaración de la Renta (IRPF, Modelo 100) annually — deadline: 30 June
  • Spanish tax residents are taxed on their worldwide income (subject to applicable double-taxation treaties)
  • Spain has agreements with 100+ countries including the USA, UK, Germany, Netherlands, France, Belgium, and Australia
  • Modelo 720: Mandatory declaration of overseas assets exceeding €50,000 per category. Filed once, updated only when values change by €20,000+.
  • Recommended: engage a local gestoría — annual fee for an individual tax return typically €150–400

Year One Admin Calendar

  • Month 1: Empadronamiento → NIE/TIE → bank account → health insurance setup
  • Month 1–2: Tarjeta SIP (health card) application at local Centro de Salud
  • Month 1–3: School enrollment confirmed; rental utilities transferred to your name
  • Month 3–6: Driving licence exchange (DGT); Social Security registration; gestoría introduction
  • Month 6–12: TIE renewal if approaching 1-year expiry; Modelo 720 review with gestoría
  • Month 12+: First Spanish IRPF income tax return (Modelo 100) filed by 30 June of the following year

Healthcare in Year One

  • Apply for your Tarjeta SIP at your nearest Centro de Salud once empadronado and with Social Security or valid coverage established
  • Register with a GP (médico de cabecera) at your nearest health centre
  • Emergency care is always available at any public hospital — free of charge regardless of registration status
  • Prescription system: most medications issued by your GP and heavily subsidised through the public system
  • Private therapy widely available: €60–120/session for English-speaking therapists
